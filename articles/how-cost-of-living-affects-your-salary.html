How Cost of Living Actually Affects Your Salary (And Why Most People Get It Wrong)

Feb 2026 10 min read

The Number on Your Paycheck Is Not Your Salary

You just received a job offer for $150,000 a year. Congratulations. But before you sign, there is a critical question most people fail to ask: where will you be spending that money?

A salary is not a fixed measure of wealth. It is a promise of purchasing power, and that purchasing power changes dramatically depending on where you live. A software engineer earning $150,000 in San Francisco has a fundamentally different financial reality than someone earning the same amount in Austin, Texas, or the equivalent in London, or Bangkok. The raw number is almost meaningless on its own.

This is the single biggest mistake people make when evaluating job offers, negotiating relocations, or comparing career paths across cities and countries. They look at the number and forget to look at what the number can buy. This article breaks down exactly how cost of living affects your real salary, how the indices that measure it actually work, and how to use that knowledge to make smarter career and financial decisions.

What Is Purchasing Power Parity?

Purchasing power parity, often abbreviated as PPP, is the economic principle that identical goods and services should theoretically cost the same in different locations once you adjust for exchange rates and local prices. Economists have been studying this concept for over a century, and it forms the foundation of every cost-of-living comparison tool in existence.

In practical terms, PPP answers a simple question: if you can afford a certain quality of life in City A on your current salary, how much would you need to earn in City B to maintain the exact same standard of living? Not just the same apartment size, but the same quality of groceries, the same dining-out frequency, the same ability to save for retirement, and the same financial breathing room at the end of each month.

A salary is not what you earn. It is what you can afford. Two people earning the same nominal amount in different cities can have wildly different qualities of life.

The gap is often larger than people expect. According to cost-of-living data aggregated from multiple sources, a person earning $150,000 in San Francisco would need roughly $95,000 in Austin to maintain the same purchasing power. That is a $55,000 difference in nominal salary for the exact same lifestyle. On the other hand, that same standard of living in New York City might require $160,000 or more, depending on which borough and neighborhood you choose.

A Real Comparison: $150K Across Four Cities

Let us trace a concrete example through four cities to see how a single salary translates across different economies. We will use $150,000 as our starting salary in San Francisco, and calculate the equivalent purchasing power in Austin, London, and Bangkok.

City COLI Index Equivalent Salary Key Factor
San Francisco 179 $150,000 High housing, high tax
Austin, TX 111 ~$93,000 No state income tax
London, UK 145 ~$97,000 (GBP equivalent) Exchange rate adds complexity
Bangkok, Thailand 52 ~$44,000 (THB equivalent) Dramatically lower costs

The numbers above illustrate a fundamental truth about global compensation. A person earning the equivalent of $44,000 in Bangkok can live at the same material standard as someone earning $150,000 in San Francisco. Their apartment might be comparable in size and quality, they can eat out at similar frequencies, and their savings rate as a percentage of income could be nearly identical. The raw number is radically different, but the purchasing power is the same.

For Austin, the story is particularly interesting for Americans evaluating domestic moves. Texas has no state income tax, which effectively gives you a raise compared to California, where state income taxes can exceed 13% at higher brackets. When you combine the lower cost of living with the tax advantage, the gap widens even further than the COLI index alone suggests.

How the Cost of Living Index (COLI) Works

The Cost of Living Index, or COLI, is a standardized measure that compares the relative expense of living in different locations. Most major indices use New York City as the baseline, setting it equal to 100. Every other city is measured relative to that baseline.

A city with a COLI of 80 means it is roughly 20% cheaper than New York. A city with a COLI of 140 means it is 40% more expensive. The indices are calculated by tracking the prices of hundreds of goods and services across categories: housing (which typically accounts for 30-40% of the total index weight), food and groceries, transportation, healthcare, utilities, and personal services.

Notable COLI Values Around the World

To give you a sense of the range, here are approximate COLI values for some commonly compared cities, with New York City at 100:

  • Zurich, Switzerland: 168 — one of the most expensive cities globally, though Swiss salaries reflect this
  • San Francisco, USA: 179 — driven almost entirely by housing costs
  • New York City, USA: 100 — the baseline
  • London, UK: 145 — high housing, but more moderate food and transport costs
  • Sydney, Australia: 110 — a balance of moderate housing and higher services
  • Austin, USA: 111 — rapidly rising but still well below coastal cities
  • Berlin, Germany: 82 — remarkably affordable for a world capital, though rising fast
  • Lisbon, Portugal: 68 — a magnet for digital nomads for good reason
  • Mexico City, Mexico: 46 — extremely favorable for remote workers on US salaries
  • Bangkok, Thailand: 52 — among the most affordable major cities in the world
  • Ho Chi Minh City, Vietnam: 42 — increasingly popular with expat tech workers

These numbers reveal the enormous range. Living in San Francisco costs roughly four times what it costs in Ho Chi Minh City. A person moving from San Francisco to Berlin could take a 54% pay cut and still maintain the same standard of living.

The Neighborhood Problem: Why City-Level Data Is Not Enough

Here is where most salary comparison tools fall short, and where careful analysis becomes essential. City-level COLI data treats an entire metropolitan area as a monolith, but anyone who has lived in a major city knows that costs vary enormously from one neighborhood to another.

Take London as an example. The COLI for London as a whole hovers around 145. But the cost of renting a one-bedroom flat in Mayfair, one of central London's most prestigious areas, might be three to four times the cost of a comparable flat in Brixton or Lewisham. If you are moving to London and plan to live in Zone 1, the city-level COLI dramatically understates your actual costs. If you plan to live in a Zone 3 or Zone 4 neighborhood with good transit links, the city-level figure may actually overstate them.

The same dynamic plays out in every major city. In New York, the difference between living in the West Village versus Astoria, Queens could be $2,000 or more per month in rent alone. In San Francisco, the Marina and Tenderloin are barely a mile apart geographically, but they occupy entirely different economic universes. In Bangkok, a serviced apartment in Sukhumvit could cost five times what a comparable local apartment costs in Lat Phrao.

When evaluating a relocation offer, do not just compare cities. Compare the specific neighborhoods where you would actually live, work, and spend your time. The variance within a single city can be as large as the variance between different countries.

This is why the best salary comparison tools allow you to drill down to neighborhood-level data rather than relying solely on city averages. Your actual cost of living depends not on where you technically reside, but on the micro-economy of your daily life.

The Currency Exchange Layer

For international moves, there is an additional factor that domestic relocations do not face: currency exchange. And this is where things can get particularly tricky.

When you move from San Francisco to London, your salary is likely quoted in British pounds. The COLI comparison tells you how much purchasing power your pounds will buy locally, but it does not account for the exchange rate between your old currency and your new one. If you have ongoing financial obligations in US dollars (student loans, a US mortgage, family support), the exchange rate directly affects your real financial position.

Exchange rates also fluctuate. The British pound might buy $1.27 today and $1.18 six months from now. For someone earning in pounds but paying debts in dollars, that 7% swing has a very real impact on their monthly budget. Similarly, a remote worker earning in dollars while living in Thailand benefits enormously when the dollar is strong against the baht, but faces a squeeze when it weakens.

This is why financial advisors who specialize in expatriate compensation recommend building a currency buffer into any international relocation calculation. A common rule of thumb is to add 5-10% to your target salary to account for exchange rate volatility, especially if you have financial obligations in your home currency.

The Formula: Putting It All Together

When you combine cost of living and currency exchange, the complete formula for calculating an equivalent salary in a new location looks like this:

Equivalent Salary = Current Salary × (Target COLI / Current COLI) × Exchange Rate

Let us walk through a worked example. Say you earn $150,000 in San Francisco (COLI: 179) and you are considering a move to London (COLI: 145). The current GBP to USD exchange rate is approximately 1.27.

First, adjust for cost of living: $150,000 × (145 / 179) = $121,508. This is the dollar amount needed in London to match your San Francisco purchasing power.

Then, convert to the local currency: $121,508 / 1.27 = approximately £95,676. That is the pound-denominated salary you should target to maintain the same standard of living.

If an employer in London offers you £80,000, you now know that you would be taking roughly a 16% effective pay cut in terms of real purchasing power compared to your San Francisco life. That might be perfectly acceptable if London offers other advantages you value, such as walkability, proximity to Europe, cultural richness, or the NHS. But at least you are making the decision with clear eyes and real numbers.

Why the Formula Is a Starting Point, Not the Answer

It is worth noting that the formula above gives you a baseline, but your personal cost of living may differ significantly from the index average. If you do not own a car, the transportation component of the index is less relevant to you. If you have children, the education and childcare components might be far more important than the index weights suggest. If you eat out frequently, restaurant costs matter more. If you cook at home, grocery prices dominate.

The best approach is to use the formula as a starting point and then adjust based on your specific spending patterns. Look at the individual category breakdowns (housing, food, transport, healthcare) and weight them according to your actual lifestyle.

Negotiating Relocation Packages: Seven Practical Tips

Armed with the right data, you can approach relocation negotiations from a position of knowledge rather than guesswork. Here are seven strategies that can make a meaningful difference in your outcome.

1. Lead with purchasing power, not nominal salary

When discussing compensation, frame the conversation around equivalent purchasing power rather than raw numbers. Saying "I need to maintain my current standard of living" is more compelling than saying "I want more money." It shifts the discussion from subjective want to objective need, and it demonstrates that you have done your homework.

2. Factor in tax differences

Many people forget that tax regimes vary enormously. Moving from Texas (0% state income tax) to California (up to 13.3%) is effectively a pay cut of thousands of dollars, even if your gross salary stays the same. International moves are even more complex. The UK has national insurance contributions, France has social charges, and some countries tax worldwide income while others use a territorial system. Always compare after-tax purchasing power, not gross salary.

3. Request a cost-of-living adjustment (COLA)

If you are relocating to a more expensive city, explicitly request a COLA as part of the offer. Many employers have formal COLA policies, especially for international assignments. If they do not, present your research showing the cost differential and propose a specific adjustment percentage.

4. Negotiate one-time relocation benefits separately

Moving costs, temporary housing, visa and legal fees, shipping personal belongings, and flights should be negotiated as separate line items. These are one-time expenses that employers often have budget for, and lumping them into salary negotiations can muddy the waters. Get them approved as distinct benefits.

5. Ask about currency protection

For international moves, some companies offer exchange rate protection clauses that peg part of your compensation to your home currency. This is especially valuable if you have financial obligations at home. Even if the company does not offer it formally, you can negotiate a salary review clause triggered by significant exchange rate movements.

6. Consider the full benefits package

In some countries, the benefits package is worth far more than in others. In the United States, employer-provided health insurance might be worth $15,000-$25,000 per year. In the UK or Canada, public healthcare means this benefit is largely irrelevant to compensation. Conversely, European employers often provide significantly more vacation time, pension contributions, and parental leave. Evaluate the total package, not just the salary line.

7. Build in a review period

Ask for a salary review after six months in your new location. By then, you will have real data on your actual living costs, and you can make a data-driven case for adjustments if the initial offer proves insufficient. Employers generally find this reasonable because it shows you are approaching the negotiation fairly.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Beyond the core mistake of ignoring cost of living entirely, there are several other traps people fall into when evaluating compensation across locations.

Anchoring on your current salary. If you earn $180,000 in New York and are offered $140,000 in Denver, it feels like a pay cut. But Denver's COLI of roughly 115 compared to New York's 100 (remember, NYC is the baseline) means $140,000 in Denver actually buys less than it seems. Always do the math rather than relying on gut feelings about whether a number seems high or low.

Ignoring savings rate. Cost of living indices measure spending, not saving. Two cities might have similar overall COLI scores, but if one has dramatically higher housing costs offset by cheaper food, your savings rate could differ significantly depending on your lifestyle. A city where housing absorbs 50% of your income leaves far less room for saving than one where housing is 30%, even if the total index is similar.

Forgetting about quality differences. A "one-bedroom apartment" in Tokyo is a very different thing than a "one-bedroom apartment" in Houston. COLI indices try to compare equivalent goods, but perfect equivalence is impossible across cultures. Factor in the qualitative differences that matter to you personally.

Using outdated data. Cost of living changes rapidly, especially in fast-growing cities like Austin, Lisbon, and Dubai. Data from even two years ago can be significantly off. Always verify your numbers against current sources before making major decisions.

Making the Decision

At the end of the day, money is only one dimension of a relocation decision. Climate, culture, language, proximity to family, career opportunities, lifestyle preferences, and personal fulfillment all matter. But money is the one dimension you can measure precisely, and failing to measure it properly is a mistake that compounds over years.

A seemingly generous offer in an expensive city might leave you living paycheck to paycheck. A modest-sounding offer in an affordable city might let you save aggressively and build wealth faster than you thought possible. The only way to know is to run the numbers.

Do not let the size of a number on a page determine the trajectory of your career. Let the purchasing power behind that number, the life it can actually buy you, guide your decisions.
